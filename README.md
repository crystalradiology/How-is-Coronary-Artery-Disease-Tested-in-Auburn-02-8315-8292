What is Coronary Artery Disease?
Coronary artery disease occurs when the arteries that supply blood to your heart become narrowed or blocked due to plaque buildup. This plaque is made up of fat, cholesterol, and calcium deposits.
•	Over time, this buildup can:
•	Reduce blood flow to the heart
•	Cause chest pain (angina)
•	Increase the risk of a heart attack
•	Lead to serious heart complications
The challenge with CAD is that it often develops without clear symptoms in the early stages.

Who Should Get Tested for CAD?
You may be at higher risk of coronary artery disease if you have:
•	A family history of heart disease
•	High blood pressure
•	High cholesterol levels
•	Diabetes
•	Obesity or excess weight
•	A history of smoking
•	A sedentary lifestyle
Even if you feel healthy, CAD can develop silently. Early screening helps detect problems before symptoms appear.

How is Coronary Artery Disease Tested?
There are several tests used to diagnose CAD, but modern imaging techniques—especially CT scans—are among the most effective and non-invasive options.
Let’s explore the most common methods.
1. Coronary Calcium Scan (CT Calcium Score)
A coronary calcium scan is a quick and non-invasive CT scan that measures calcium deposits in your coronary arteries.
Benefits:
•	Detects early plaque buildup
•	Provides a calcium score to assess risk
•	No injections required
•	Takes only a few minutes
This scan is ideal for people who may not have symptoms but are at risk.
Patients often search for Coronary Artery Disease near me to access this simple and effective screening tool.
2. Heart CT Scan (CT Coronary Angiography)
A heart CT scan provides detailed images of your coronary arteries and blood flow.
In some cases, a contrast dye is used to clearly visualise blockages. This is called CT coronary angiography.
This scan helps:
•	Identify narrowed or blocked arteries
•	Assess the severity of plaque buildup
•	Detect early-stage coronary artery disease
At Crystal Radiology, advanced CT technology ensures accurate and safe imaging for heart health assessment.
3. Blood Tests
Blood tests can help identify risk factors for CAD.
These include:
•	Cholesterol levels
•	Blood sugar levels
•	C-reactive protein (CRP), which indicates inflammation
While blood tests don’t show blockages directly, they help assess your overall risk.
4. Electrocardiogram (ECG)
An ECG measures the electrical activity of your heart.
It can:
•	Detect irregular heart rhythms
•	Show signs of a past or ongoing heart attack
•	Electrodes are placed on your chest, arms, and legs to record heart activity.
5. Stress Test
A stress test evaluates how your heart performs during physical activity or simulated stress.
It helps:
•	Identify reduced blood flow to the heart
•	Detect hidden heart issues during exertion
Your heart is monitored using ECG or imaging while you exercise or receive medication to increase heart activity.
